The evidence before launch

A demo is useful. A pass/fail test plan is more useful.

Each scenario should prove one approved behavior. A failed scenario stays visible and gets fixed or routed to a person before the coverage expands.

Risk questions

Reliability is a process you can inspect—not a slogan.

Is this already proven with paying pest-control clients?

No verified customer result is claimed here. Elixis is in early customer validation. Current evidence is the working product demo, configuration process, test scenarios, and backend call records.

Can you guarantee it will never make a mistake?

No. AI can encounter unexpected language and edge cases. The launch process reduces risk with narrow rules, tests, monitoring, summaries, and human handoffs; it cannot honestly promise perfection.

What happens when a caller asks for a person?

The call follows the human-request rule your company approves—transfer, callback capture, or another defined route. Elixis should not trap a caller in automation.

Will this replace our current phone setup?

Not automatically. The audit maps your numbers, carrier, staff, answering service, booking system, and uncovered windows before recommending a routing design.

Can we change answers after launch?

Yes. Approved knowledge and routing rules should be reviewed as services, hours, staff, or policies change. The exact update workflow is defined during implementation.

Why ask about call volume?

Volume changes architecture, review workload, and cost. A rough band is enough for the first fit check; no call records are needed in this form.
